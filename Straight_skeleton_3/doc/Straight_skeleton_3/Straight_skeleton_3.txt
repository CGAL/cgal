\section Straight_skeleton_3Introduction Introduction

\subsection Straight_skeleton_3Straight Straight Skeletons

This package implements weighted straight skeletons of three-dimensional polyhedra.
An intuitive way to think of the construction of straight skeletons is to imagine that wavefronts
(or grassfires) are spawned at each face of the polyhedron, and are moving inward, parallel
to the face. As the fronts progress, the shapes of faces change depending on the angles formed
between polyhedral faces, and topological changes happen as faces collide or disappear.
Under this transformation, polyhedral edges move along the angular bisector of the planes
subtending their incident faces, tracing a partition of the interior of the polyhedron:
the straight skeleton.

\section Straight_skeleton_3Definitions Definitions

We now give precise definitions of the various concepts relevant to this package.

\subsection Straight_skeleton_3PolyhedronDef Polyhedron

The definition of polyhedron in use here is slightly more generic than what is usually found
in the rest of \cgal, for example in \ref PkgSurfaceMesh, \ref PkgPolyhedron,
or \ref PkgPolygonMeshProcessing.

We first define a <em>convex polyhedron</em>, as a bounded, non-empty intersection of a finite
number of halfspaces of the Euclidean three-space \f$ \mathbb{R}^3\f$. A <em>polyhedron</em> is then
a bounded subset of \f$ \mathbb{R}^3\f$ which can be expressed as the finite union of convex polyhedra.
A polyhedron can thus have holes, handles, disconnected connected components, etc.

The boundary of such polyhedron can be represented as a structural complex, which consists of vertices V,
edges E, faces F, and symmetric incidence relations between these elements \cgalCite{k-ugpdd-99}.
Vertices represent points in \f$ \mathbb{R}^3\f$. Edges are straight line segments between two points.
Faces are planar, possibly non-convex polygons, and possibly with holes.

With the definition above, a polyhedron forms 2-manifold structure in a combinatorial sense,
but it is not required to be geometrically manifold.
For example, faces are connected, but are not required to be simply connected.
Moreover, there might exist so-called touching elements: for example, a vertex or an edge touching the
interior of a non-incident face. In other words, the boundary complex of a polytope needs not be face-to-face
This is thus a major relaxation in terms of self-intersections compared to numerous other algorithms.

A significant limitation is however present in the current implementation: the polyhedron must be
in a general position, i.e. all combinations of three planes must intersect in a single point.
We refer to \ref Straight_skeleton_3Limitations for more details.

\subsection Straight_skeleton_3StraightSkeletonDef Straight Skeleton

We call the structural elements of an input polyhedron <em>contour elements</em>.
Given a contour face called the <em>source face</em>, its <em>offset face</em> at time `t` is a face
parallel and with the same orientation such that the Euclidean distance between the planes
supporting both faces is exactly `t`. An offset face is always located to the bounded side of
its source face, which is an oriented plane.

The straight skeleton is spawned by applying a continuous, <I>inward offsetting</I> to the contour faces.
Under this transformation, polyhedral vertices (edges) move along the angular trisector (bisector)
of the planes subtending the incident faces, at a speed that depends on the angle between
the three (two) incident contour faces. As elements shift, topological changes, also called
<em>events</em>, may happen, such as two moving vertices impacting each other, or when a moving vertex
collides with a moving face. When an event happens, the combinatorial structure of the polyhedron
must be modified as to remain a valid polyhedron.

The straight skeleton is the set of faces traced out by the moving edges during this process.
It forms a unique partition of the interior of the polyhedron into <I>straight skeleton regions</I>,
corresponding to the monotone areas traced by the continuous inward offsetting of the contour faces.
Each region corresponds to exactly one contour face. These regions are bounded by the angular bisectors
of the supporting planes of the contour faces, and each such region is itself a non-convex,
weakly simple polyhedron [WEAKLY SIMPLE POLYTOPE ARE NOT DEFINED].

\cgalFigureBegin{SLS3Skeletons,simple_skeletons_3.png}

Constructing the exterior skeleton of the outer contour is achieved by reversing the orientation
of the polyhedral faces, and shifting outwards. Note that the exterior skeleton is unbounded.

\subsection Straight_skeleton_3WeightedStraightSkeletonDef Weighted Straight Skeleton

Weighted straight skeletons are a generalization of straight skeletons: contour faces are assigned
a weight, which can be understood as assigning a speed to the wavefront spawned from
the contour face. Vertices (edges) no longer move along the angular trisectors (bisectors)
between three (two) contour faces, but along a <em>weighted</em> trisector (bisector).

This \cgal package supports positive multiplicative weights: if the supporting plane of a contour face
is described through the equation `ax+by+cz+d=0`, then the supporting plane of the offset face
at distance `t` is `ax+by+cz+d-t=0`. With a multiplicative weight `w`, the equation becomes `ax+by+cz+d-wt=0`.
Therefore, a larger weight implies a faster moving front.

A significant change from unweighted straight skeleton of polyhedra is that the faces
of a weighted straight skeleton of a polyhedron with holes are no longer necessarily weakly simple polyhedra:
a face can for example completely encompass a set of faces incident to a hole, giving it the topology
of a ball with a void.

\subsection Straight_skeleton_32D3DComparison Comparison with Two-dimensional Straight Skeletons

Although the definition of straight skeleton as shifting elements of a polytope is identical
in 2D and 3D, some significant differences exist between the two settings.

The first difference appears immediately as one starts shifting faces in 3D: while a vertex in 2D
has only 2 incident edges, a vertex in 3D has an arbitrary number of incident faces.
If the number of faces incident to a vertex is greater than 3, we call it a <em>high-degree</em> vertex.
When 2D edges shift, the vertex remains well defined as the intersection of the offset edges,
but when the 3D faces of a high-degree vertex shift, they no longer intersect in a single point
if the input polyhedron is in general position (see also \ref Straight_skeleton_3Limitations).
Instead, a high-degree vertex will split into a graph of degree 3 vertices whose embedding
is free of self-intersections. It is therefore necessary to construct such graphs for all high-degree
vertices <em>before</em> starting the process of construction of the skeleton.
The current approach is to consider all possible combinations of incident planes
that form a degree 3 graph, i.e. to explore unrooted binary trees, and considering whether their geometrical
embedding is valid \cgalCite{cgal:aw-ssmonp-16}, \cgalCite{cgal:b-mbras-10}, \cgalCite{cgal:rha-fopmv-16}.
As it turns out, there may even exist multiple valid combinations, which means that an arbitarary choice
must be made.

The ambiguity steeming from the arbitrary choice of vertex split can also appear during event treatment:

The conclusion from these observations is that contrary to (unweighted) 2D straight skeletons,
the straight skeleton and the face offsetting of a polyhedron are not uniquely defined.

\section Straight_skeleton_3PolyhedronOffsetting Polyhedron Offsetting

The straight skeleton is defined by the trace of the moving edges, but one can also consider
the state of the polyhedron at a fixed time `t` made up of the translated elements.
This is the <I>offset polyhedron</I>. We call the process of computing an offset polyhedron
<em>polyhedron face offsetting</em>, or simply <em>polyhedron offsetting</em>.

An offset polyhedron can have fewer, equal, or more faces as its source polyhedron.
It can even be composed of multiple polyhedra. If the source polyhedron has no holes,
then no offset polyhedron has any holes.
If the source polyhedron has holes, any of the offset polyhedra can have holes itself, but it might
as well have no holes at all (e.g., if the distance is sufficiently large). Each offset polyhedron
has the same orientation as the source polyhedron.

Outward offsetting, similarly to exterior straight skeletons, are achieved by reversing the orientation of
the faces of the input polyhedron.

\section Straight_skeleton_3Implementation Implementation

The algorithm implemented in this package is an extension of the method used
in \ref PkgStraightSkeleton2Ref: future modifications of the combinatorial structure
of the polyhedron, called <em>events</em>, are detected and treated in a chronological order.
While in 2D there are only 2 event types, edge events and split events, there exists 13 different
event types in 3D, which can be regrouped in two categories: <em>vanish events</em>,
and <em>contact events</em>. Vanish events are local combinatorial changes steeming from the collapse of
edges (thus akin to the edge event in 2D); contact events are interaction with non-local elements
of the mesh such as a vertex piercing a face (thus akin to split events in 2D).
We refer to \cgalCite{cgal:w-sspp-15} and \cgalCite{cgal:aw-ssmonp-16} for algorithmic details.

Contrary to the 2D, the extraction of an offset polyhedron at a given time `t` is more tedious
than simply drawing an isoline at height `t` of an existing straight skeleton. As such,
functions of this package compute the straight skeleton <em>and</em> the offset polyhedron
simultaneously.

The main function to construct an offset polyhedron is `CGAL::Straight_skeletons_3::face_offset()`.
It enables building multiple offset polyhedra without recomputing the straight skeleton.
In addition to the named parameters of this function, fine tuning of the algorithm is available
to advanced users through a configuration file, see the class `CGAL::Straight_skeletons_3::Configuration`.

\subsection Straight_skeleton_3Limitations Limitations

Two limitations exist in the current implementation.

Firstly, as explained in \ref Straight_skeleton_32D3DComparison, the very first step of the algorithm is to split high-degree
vertices, i.e. to construct for each such vertex a graph of degree 3 vertices whose embedding
is free of self-intersections. The current implementation uses a brute-force approach, trying all
combinations until a valid one is found. Unfortunately, the number of combinations to try is given
by the Catalan number C_n, with n = deg(v) - 2, which thus grows exponentially with respect
to the degree of the vertex. In practice, this means that for a vertex with degree greater than 10,
the splitting process will start to become noticeable; above 15, it is very unlikely that the algorithm
will finish in any amount of time. Note however that the input polyhedron needs not to be triangulated,
and a preprocessing step that merges almost-coplanar faces is also performed automatically,
which means that only having a high degree of unique incident planes makes a vertex problematic.

\cgalFigureAnchor{SLS3HighDegreeVertices}
<center>
<img src="sls_high_degrees.png" style="max-width:40%;"/>
</center>
\cgalFigureCaptionBegin{SLS3HighDegreeVertices}
Some high degree vertices are problematic; others are not because of coplanar face merging.
\cgalFigureCaptionEnd

A second, significant limitation is that the algorithm can only be applied to polyhedra in
generic position. In this context, this means that no three faces shall intersect in more than
a single point. This excludes for example coplanar or parallel faces. The reason for this limitation is
that the family of events is no longer sufficient to describe all modifications of the mesh,
which can then become significantly non-local such as entire faces merging together at the same
time. To circumvent this limitation, we automatically apply an epsilon tilt of the planar faces.
Oftentimes, this perturbation is transparent as it is lost in the numerical errors that necessarily
arise while doing inexact square roots.

\subsection Straight_skeleton_3TraitsChoice Kernel Choice

The very basis of straight skeleton construction is the computation of the intersection of moving
elements. In 3D, this central operation is the intersection of four (shifting) supporting planes,
answering the question: when and where will these four planes intersect in a single point. The answers
are the time and position of an event involving these four faces. For the computation to be correct,
these planes must be normalized. This inevitably introduces square root computations.

Consequently, the current algorithm requires a kernel providing <em>at least</em> exact predicates
and exact constructions, possibly with exact square roots. Due to the mandatory perturbation mechanisms
described in \ref Straight_skeleton_3Limitations, the use of a traits providing exact square root computations
is not recommended: a (tiny) numerical error in the normalization of planes is anyway indistinguishable
from the perturbation. Exact constructions are required to ensure that all vertices lay exactly
on the supporting planes of their respective incident faces.
Thus, we advise the use of the kernel `CGAL::Exact_predicates_exact_constructions_kernel`.
